# 11. Limits of the Method

The workflow has limits.

Computer algebra verification is not formal proof. It can confirm algebraic
identities, explore ansatz families, and catch coefficient errors, but it does
not replace a fully quantified analytic proof or an interactive theorem prover
where such rigor is required.

Language models can bias framing. They may choose convenient formalizations,
miss alternatives, overfit to the user's language, or produce persuasive but
wrong explanations. The workflow reduces this risk; it does not eliminate it.

The human owner remains responsible for postulate choices. A bad postulate can
be explicit and fenced while still being physically unmotivated or false.
Methodological hygiene is not a substitute for scientific judgment.

Archive discipline costs time. Charters, scripts, reports, downgrade banners,
and provenance ledgers slow the work. This cost is real, but it is the cost of
making AI-assisted theory auditable.

Public artifacts create social and publication complications. A public archive
helps credibility but can conflict with anonymous review, expose embarrassing
dead ends, and require careful disclosure of AI assistance.

Finally, the method is better at preventing quiet rationalization than at
guaranteeing discovery. A disciplined workflow can still end in a false theory.
Its promise is that the route to failure becomes clearer.
