# 1. Introduction

Large language models can generate plausible theoretical arguments quickly.
That is useful, but it is also dangerous. In theoretical physics, a fluent
derivation can be worse than no derivation if it hides assumptions, patches
failed claims, or persuades a researcher to protect a preferred idea.

The central claim of this paper is that useful AI-assisted theory development
depends less on model intelligence than on workflow discipline. A language
model should not be treated as an oracle. It should be placed inside an
adversarial process where candidates face explicit gates, algebraic claims are
checked by executable scripts when possible, postulates are separated from
theorems, and failed ideas remain visible.

We describe such a workflow and analyze a case study: a vacuum-substance
gravity program whose gravitational sector ultimately closed on the Einstein
field equations. This outcome matters methodologically because it was not the
exciting target. The program began by investigating non-GR vacuum-energy
dynamics. The adversarial workflow instead killed many candidate deviations
and forced the surviving gravitational response to match GR.

This paper is not a claim that AI systems can produce trustworthy physics by
themselves. It is also not a claim that computer algebra scripts are formal
proofs. The contribution is a practical workflow between informal reasoning
and full formal verification: charters, executable derivation checks,
dependency ledgers, adoption fences, downgrade records, and provenance grades.

The paper should be readable even if the reader rejects the underlying
vacuum-substance theory. The object of study is the process: how an AI-assisted
theory program can be structured so that failure is recorded rather than
rationalized.
