# 7. Workflow Component 4: Honest Downgrades and Supersession

AI-assisted work produces many provisional estimates. Some later become
theorems. Some are replaced by better calculations. Some fail. The archive
should preserve this history rather than editing it away.

The case-study norm is to use supersession banners and downgrade records. When
an estimate is replaced by a derivation, the old document is not silently
rewritten as if it had always known the answer. It is marked as superseded,
demoted, or historical, and the replacement is linked.

This serves three purposes.

First, it protects downstream readers. They can see which statements are live
theory content and which are historical scaffolding.

Second, it protects the process from hindsight laundering. Failed or weakened
claims remain visible.

Third, it gives a quality signal. A theory workflow that records downgrades is
less likely to be merely protecting its preferred story.

Examples include the demotion of earlier recovery-shaped postulates once their
content was derived downstream, and speculative mechanisms that were killed
rather than patched after budget, sign, or stability gates failed.
