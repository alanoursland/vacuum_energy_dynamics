# Consequence: Gravitational Waves

---

## What This Document Is

This is a consequence document. It traces what Postulates 4 and 5 commit us to about gravitational waves. The result is qualitative: gravitational waves exist and carry energy. The framework does not yet support quantitative predictions about wave emission rates, polarization structure, or propagation speed, but the qualitative existence-and-energy-carrying result follows from the postulates we have committed to.

General relativity predicts gravitational waves from linearizing Einstein's equations around a background solution [Einstein, 1916; Einstein, 1918]. The Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar provided indirect confirmation through observed orbital decay matching the quadrupole formula's prediction [Hulse & Taylor, 1975; Taylor & Weisberg, 1982]. Direct detection came with LIGO's observation of GW150914 in 2015 [Abbott et al., 2016] and subsequent detections including binary neutron star and black hole mergers. Any framework attempting to account for gravitational phenomena must predict gravitational waves carrying energy.

Our framework does so. This document shows how the prediction follows from the postulates.

---

## The Argument

The argument combines Postulate 4 (curvature contains energy) and Postulate 5 (minimum-energy configuration, with changes propagating).

From Postulate 5: when a constraint on the vacuum changes — such as when a mass moves, accelerates, or reconfigures — the minimum-energy configuration changes too. The vacuum must adjust from the old minimum to the new one. Because no transition can be instantaneous across all of space, the adjustment propagates from the location of the change outward.

For slow changes, the propagation reaches each location before the next change arrives, and the vacuum remains approximately at minimum throughout. For rapid changes, the propagation cannot keep pace. Regions near the change find themselves away from minimum because the change is already happening there while earlier adjustments are still in transit elsewhere. The not-at-minimum configuration spreads outward as the change's influence spreads.

From Postulate 4: any configuration that is not at minimum has positive configuration energy. The departing-from-minimum regions are therefore regions of stored configuration energy.

Combining: propagating not-at-minimum configurations carry configuration energy outward from the source. These propagating energetic disturbances are gravitational waves.

Existence follows from the propagation requirement (Postulate 5). Energy-carrying follows from configuration being energetic (Postulate 4). The two postulates together produce the result.

## What the Waves Are

Under our framework, gravitational waves are propagating changes in the vacuum's configuration — or equivalently, propagating changes in spacetime curvature, since curvature is how the vacuum's configuration appears geometrically. They are not oscillations of some separate "gravitational field" sitting on top of spacetime. They are waves *in* spacetime itself, since spacetime is the vacuum and the vacuum's configuration is what is changing.

This framing differs slightly from how gravitational waves are usually described in general relativity. In GR, waves are oscillations of the metric — the mathematical object encoding spacetime geometry. In our framework, waves are oscillations of the vacuum's configuration, with the metric-level description being a particular angle on the underlying vacuum-level phenomenon. These are equivalent at the level of observable predictions but differ in what they say the waves fundamentally are.

The identification "gravitational waves are curvature waves" is made in Postulate 5's document. Standard terminology uses "gravitational waves"; our framework's language uses "curvature waves." Both terms refer to the same physical phenomenon.

## Energy Carried by the Waves

The energy carried by a gravitational wave is configuration energy (Postulate 4's terminology) associated with the wave's not-at-minimum configuration. As the wave propagates, it carries this energy away from the source.

Where this energy comes from is addressed in Postulate 5's document and in the redshift proof. When a system radiates gravitational waves, the source system loses energy in an amount equal to the energy carried by the waves. The source's configuration returns closer to its local minimum as the wave departs; the wave carries the "excess" energy outward.

For a binary system in decaying orbit, this energy comes from the system's orbital energy. As the orbit decays, the system's total energy decreases by the amount radiated. The Hulse-Taylor observations confirm this: the binary pulsar's orbit shrinks at a rate matching what the quadrupole formula predicts for energy loss to gravitational radiation.

Our framework predicts the qualitative result — energy loss by the source equal to energy carried by the waves — but does not derive the quantitative rate. The quadrupole formula is a specific prediction of GR that depends on the detailed wave-emission dynamics. Whether our framework reproduces the quadrupole formula, or produces a different prediction, depends on the mathematical structure of the vacuum, which remains an open question.

## What the Framework Does Not Predict Yet

Several quantitative aspects of gravitational waves are standard predictions of GR that our framework cannot yet reproduce or check.

**The wave equation.** GR's linearized field equations give a specific wave equation for metric perturbations, with propagation at speed $c$. Our framework commits to waves existing and propagating at some speed constrained by $c$ (through Postulate 1's implications about information propagation), but does not derive a specific wave equation. Doing so would require the vacuum's mathematical structure.

**Polarization structure.** GR predicts gravitational waves have two polarization modes, typically called "plus" and "cross," both transverse to the direction of propagation. Our framework does not yet predict wave polarization. This is particularly interesting because the polarization structure is a strong discriminator between gravitational theories — any framework that predicts different polarizations (additional "breathing" modes, for instance) would conflict with observations. Whether our framework predicts the GR polarizations, or something different, is among the most important open quantitative questions.

**The quadrupole formula.** GR gives a specific formula for the power radiated by a source as gravitational waves, depending on the second time derivative of the source's mass quadrupole moment. Our framework does not yet reproduce this formula. Whether it could, and whether its prediction matches GR's, is unresolved.

**Propagation speed.** GR predicts gravitational waves propagate at exactly the speed of light. The GW170817 event (a binary neutron star merger) confirmed this by showing gravitational waves and gamma-ray photons arriving within about 1.7 seconds of each other after traveling 130 million light-years, limiting any speed difference to one part in $10^{15}$ or better [Abbott et al., 2017]. Our framework expects the same result but cannot derive it rigorously without further machinery.

**Coupling to matter.** GR specifies how gravitational waves interact with matter they pass through (causing tidal strains that LIGO detects). Our framework would need to work out this coupling from its postulates, which involves understanding how vacuum configuration changes affect matter distributions.

## What This Consequence Shows

The consequence is qualitative but not trivial. Several things become clear from combining Postulates 4 and 5 in this way.

Gravitational waves are a derived prediction of our framework, not a separate postulate. We did not build them in. They follow from the combination of configuration-carries-energy and minimum-configuration-with-propagating-changes.

The framework's account of what waves are is physically concrete. Under GR, "what gravitational waves are" is sometimes debated — are they oscillations in a physical field, or mere coordinate artifacts? LIGO's direct detection settled this empirically, but the interpretive question in GR remained open. Our framework gives an unambiguous physical account: waves are propagating configurations of the vacuum, which is a physical substance (energy, per the overview). There is no ambiguity about whether they are "real."

The framework's qualitative agreement with observation is established for gravitational waves. This joins gravitational redshift as a well-verified phenomenon the framework reproduces. The next step is quantitative — can the framework match GR's numerical predictions, or does it diverge?

That quantitative question is beyond what this consequence document addresses. But the qualitative agreement is a real result.
