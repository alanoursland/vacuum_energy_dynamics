# Postulate 5: Minimum-Energy Configuration

---

## Statement

**Postulate 5 (Minimum-Energy Configuration).** Given a set of constraints — mass distribution, boundary conditions, and any other physical conditions present — the vacuum assumes the configuration that minimizes total configuration energy consistent with those constraints. Configurations disturbed from the minimum are not stable; they relax toward the minimum. Because no transition can be instantaneous across all of space, changes in the minimum-energy configuration propagate from where they originate outward, and rapid changes produce propagating disturbances we observe as gravitational waves.

---

## What the Postulate Says

Building on Postulate 4 (configurations have energies), this postulate specifies the dynamics. Among all configurations consistent with a given set of constraints, the vacuum settles into whichever has the least configuration energy. If disturbed from that minimum, it relaxes back. Since no transition can happen instantaneously across all of space, relaxation propagates.

Three claims, each doing specific work.

First, vacuum configures toward minimum energy. This is a variational principle. The vacuum does not assume arbitrary shapes; it assumes the specific shape that minimizes total configuration energy given whatever constraints are in place. Masses are constraints. Boundary conditions are constraints. The shape the vacuum takes is the optimization result given all constraints.

Second, disturbances are not stable. A configuration that is not at minimum energy will evolve. The direction of evolution is toward minimum. This gives the framework a dynamical principle: if you know the constraints and the current configuration, you know (in principle) how the configuration will change.

Third, changes in the configuration propagate. When the minimum changes at some location — because the constraint has changed there — the rest of space cannot adjust instantaneously. The transition from the old minimum to the new one must spread across space from the originating location. For slow changes, this propagation keeps up and the vacuum remains approximately at minimum throughout. For rapid changes, the propagation cannot keep pace, leaving regions temporarily away from minimum; these non-minimum configurations propagate outward as waves carrying configuration energy. What we observe as gravitational waves is this propagation of incomplete relaxation.

## Gravity Wells as Minimum-Energy Configurations

A static mass sits in a region of vacuum. Its presence is a constraint on the vacuum — some amount of vacuum has been locally reduced. The framework does not yet explain why mass reduces vacuum in the first place; this is the same open question as the force mechanism from Postulate 3. What the framework does commit to is that once a mass exists, its presence acts as a constraint on the surrounding vacuum configuration.

Under Postulate 5, the vacuum settles into whichever configuration minimizes total configuration energy given that constraint. That configuration is the gravity well shape. The well is not a rigid accessory dragged around by the mass — it is the answer to an optimization problem, continuously re-solved as the constraint's location (the mass's position) changes.

A consequence worth stating explicitly: gravity wells do not fill in on their own, even though flat space has lower configuration energy than curved space. This is because "minimum-energy configuration" is always minimum *given the constraints*. Flat space is the minimum-energy configuration for a region without mass. A gravity well is the minimum-energy configuration for a region with mass. As long as the mass persists — which, by conservation of its rest mass-energy, means as long as the mass has not been converted into something else that departs the region — the constraint persists, and the well persists as the constrained minimum. If the mass annihilates with an antimass and radiates its energy away as photons, the constraint lifts, and the vacuum can relax toward flat. But in the absence of such a process, the well is stable.

Different mass distributions produce different wells. A spherically symmetric mass produces a spherically symmetric well. An elongated mass (a rod) produces a more complex shape, because the constraint distribution is different. In each case, the well is whatever configuration minimizes energy given the specific distribution of vacuum reduction the mass represents. The integration over the mass's extent determines the detailed shape, but the principle is the same: minimum energy given the constraints.

This is analogous to how general relativity handles the problem through Einstein's equations — both frameworks arrive at specific field configurations determined by the mass distribution, though through different formal machinery. Our framework posits minimum-energy configuration as the underlying principle; GR posits the Einstein-Hilbert action as the thing being extremized. Whether these approaches are ultimately equivalent or produce different predictions in some regime is an open question.

## Gravitational Waves as Propagating Relaxation

When the constraint changes — when a mass moves, accelerates, or reconfigures — the minimum-energy configuration changes too. The vacuum must rearrange to match the new constraint.

A change in the minimum-energy configuration cannot be instantaneously realized everywhere. The transition from the old minimum to the new one must propagate from where the change originated outward across space. This is not an additional claim beyond the minimum-energy principle; it is a consequence of it. If the minimum changes at some location, the rest of space can only learn of the change and respond to it through some process that propagates. No process is instantaneous.

If the constraint changes slowly, the propagation reaches each location before the next change arrives. The configuration at each location has time to settle into the current minimum before anything new happens. The vacuum remains approximately at minimum throughout, and there are no observable propagating disturbances beyond the quiet transition itself. This is the adiabatic limit.

If the constraint changes rapidly, the propagation cannot keep pace. A region near the change finds itself away from minimum because the change is already happening there while earlier adjustments are still in transit elsewhere. The region's configuration departs from the local minimum, and this departure itself carries configuration energy. The departure propagates outward, spreading as the information about the change spreads.

Those propagating disturbances are gravitational waves — or, in our framework's language, curvature waves, since they are literally propagating changes in the vacuum's curvature. They carry configuration energy from the disturbed region to the wider space. The energy was stored in the temporary non-minimum configuration; as the system relaxes, the energy redistributes through the propagation itself.

When a configuration transitions from a higher-energy state to a lower-energy state, the released energy has two possible destinations. Some of it can convert back to vacuum energy — the region that was curved becomes less curved, and the configuration energy stored in the curvature is absorbed into the vacuum as the configuration relaxes locally. The rest can radiate outward as curvature waves, carrying configuration energy away from the region. The split between these two outcomes depends on the dynamics of the relaxation. Smooth, matched relaxation converts more of the released energy back to vacuum; mismatched or rapid relaxation radiates more as waves. In the adiabatic limit, essentially all released energy returns to vacuum; in highly dynamic situations like binary mergers, a substantial fraction radiates. The two outcomes are not alternatives in the usual sense — most real situations involve both, with the ratio determined by how closely the relaxation dynamics match the rate at which the constraint is changing.

This picture makes several predictions consistent with general relativity. Free-falling masses (geodesic motion) do not radiate, because their motion is the smooth evolution of the constraint toward minimum at each point; propagation of minor readjustments keeps up with the motion, and no excess accumulates. Accelerating masses radiate, because the constraint is changing faster than propagation can fully accommodate, and the resulting non-minimum configuration propagates outward as waves. Orbital systems radiate because the configuration near the orbiting masses cannot perfectly track the orbit's dynamics, and small residuals propagate outward, leading to orbital decay over time.

The match with GR is qualitative here. Whether our framework produces the correct quadrupole formula for wave emission, and whether it matches GR's polarization structure for the waves, are open quantitative questions.

## The Energy Accounting

Combined with Postulate 3 (vacuum exchange during gradient traversal), this postulate gives the framework an account of energy flow in gravitational processes.

When a mass moves through a gradient, vacuum is consumed (Postulate 3). The consumed vacuum's energy has potential destinations: kinetic energy of the moving mass, or configuration energy stored in disturbances to the surrounding vacuum that will eventually relax. When the configuration later relaxes from its disturbed state toward minimum, the released configuration energy has two further destinations — it can convert back to vacuum energy as the region returns to the minimum-energy configuration, or it can radiate outward as curvature waves.

For a mass in free-fall — the natural motion in a gradient, with the vacuum smoothly accommodating the mass's trajectory — essentially all the consumption goes to kinetic energy. Any disturbances to the configuration as the mass moves relax smoothly and return their energy to the vacuum. No waves are produced.

For a mass accelerated non-geodesically — say by an external force — the mass moves in a way that does not match what the constraint would permit at minimum energy. The vacuum is continually rearranging away from minimum, and the resulting disturbances cannot all be absorbed back as vacuum. Some portion radiates outward as waves.

For a mass in a binary orbit — where the orbiting motion cannot be quite free-fall because each mass's well distorts the other's environment — small residual non-geodesic accelerations produce small wave emission. Most of the disturbance energy returns to vacuum as the configurations follow the orbit; a small fraction radiates. Over time, the orbit decays as the system loses the radiated portion, while the returned portion is continuously recycled.

This is consistent with what general relativity predicts and what gravitational-wave observations confirm, though we have not derived the quantitative details of the split between vacuum-absorbed and wave-radiated energy.

## What the Postulate Does Not Do

**It does not give a formula for configuration energy.** Like Postulate 4, it commits to the existence and structure of configuration energy without specifying how to calculate it for a given configuration.

**It does not specify the propagation speed.** The postulate commits to changes propagating, because no transition can be instantaneous. It does not commit to a specific speed of propagation. The expected speed ($c$) follows from the identity postulate's implications about information propagation in the vacuum, but deriving this formally requires a rigorous account of what counts as a propagating substrate change — a question the framework has not yet resolved.

**It does not specify wave polarization or mode structure.** Gravitational waves in general relativity have specific polarization characteristics (plus and cross modes, both transverse). Whether our framework produces the same polarization structure depends on the mathematical structure of the vacuum, which remains open.

**It does not resolve the cosmological case.** The expanding universe is not obviously a minimum-energy configuration — it is an evolving state. Whether the expansion is compatible with Postulate 5 (perhaps as the minimum-energy configuration given cosmological boundary conditions), or whether it represents a departure from the principle, is unresolved. The cosmic expansion consequence document will need revisiting in light of this postulate.

**It does not address how mass formation produces gradients.** The postulate assumes mass distributions are given as constraints and describes how vacuum responds. How mass comes to exist with a specific spatial distribution, and how its presence becomes a vacuum-reducing constraint, is the same open question we have flagged elsewhere.

## Status and What Remains

Postulates 4 and 5 together give the framework a variational principle: configurations have energies, and systems settle into minimum-energy configurations given their constraints, with deviations propagating as waves. This is a specific commitment that lets the framework talk about static wells, wave emission, and the energy balance of gravitational phenomena in ways that were previously fuzzy.

Significant work remains. The quantitative structure of configuration energy is undetermined. The connection to the Einstein field equations is not established. The force mechanism remains open — Postulates 4 and 5 tell us about configurations and their dynamics but do not give a microscopic account of why a mass feels force in a gradient. And various cosmological questions, including how this framework handles the expanding universe, need further development.

What the postulates give us is a principled picture of what the framework is committing to about gravitational dynamics. Configurations carry energy, systems minimize that energy given constraints, and deviations produce waves. Whether this picture can be developed into a predictive theory matching general relativity in its well-tested regimes — and whether it predicts anything distinctive beyond GR — is work for the framework's future.
