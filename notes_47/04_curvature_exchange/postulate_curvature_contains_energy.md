# Postulate 4: Curvature Contains Energy

---

## Statement

**Postulate 4 (Curvature Contains Energy).** The configuration of the vacuum carries energy, measured from a baseline of flat vacuum at zero configuration energy. Any departure from flat — any curvature, any deformation — has positive configuration energy. Infinite curvature, whether as an infinite curvature value or as a discontinuous change in curvature, requires infinite configuration energy and is therefore not physically realizable. Configuration energy is a separate contribution from the energy density of the vacuum (Postulate 1) and counts toward the total energy of a region.

---

## What the Postulate Says

The postulate commits to curvature being energetic. A curved region of vacuum has more energy in it than a flat region would, not because its density is higher (Postulate 1 forbids density variation) but because its configuration carries energy on top of whatever the vacuum would contribute at its flat baseline.

Flat vacuum is the reference point. A region of flat vacuum has energy content determined only by its total amount of vacuum times the energy density. Call this the baseline energy. A region with the same amount of vacuum but arranged in a curved configuration has the baseline energy plus an additional positive contribution from the configuration itself.

The postulate commits to four specific things.

First, flat is the zero point for configuration energy. A region that is perfectly flat has no configuration energy. All the energy it contains is from the vacuum's density times its extent.

Second, any departure from flat costs energy. There is no curved configuration that has less energy than flat. You cannot get energy for free by curving the vacuum; curvature always adds.

Third, infinities in curvature cost infinite energy. A point or region where the curvature value goes to infinity (a singularity) requires infinite configuration energy. A surface where the curvature changes discontinuously from one finite value to another (a sharp edge) also requires infinite configuration energy, because the transition has infinite derivatives. Since infinite energy is not available, such configurations are not physically realizable. Physical configurations must be smooth, with both finite curvature values and finite rates of change in curvature.

Fourth, configuration energy is distinct from the density-times-amount contribution. These are two separate ways the vacuum stores energy, and both count toward the total energy of a region.

## What This Buys the Framework

Several things now become coherent that were fuzzy before.

Gravity wells contain energy. A static gravitational well around a mass is not just a geometric feature of spacetime — it is a region of stored energy. The energy is stored in the configuration of the surrounding vacuum. This explains how a well can transfer energy to falling objects: the well releases some of its stored configuration energy as the object descends, alongside the vacuum consumption that Postulate 3 describes.

Gravitational waves can carry energy. A propagating deformation in the vacuum is a changing configuration, and under this postulate, configurations have energy. A wave moving outward from a disturbed region carries configuration energy away from that region.

Energy accounting for gravitational phenomena becomes physically grounded. Standard physics is famously awkward about where gravitational energy lives — the energy of a gravitational field is coordinate-dependent and hard to localize in general relativity. Our framework takes a specific stance: the energy is in the configuration. Not distributed according to some pseudo-tensor that varies with coordinates, but literally stored in the vacuum's arrangement.

The "where does the energy come from" question for wave propagation gets an answer. When a system radiates gravitational waves, those waves are changing configurations propagating outward. The energy they carry is configuration energy that was previously stored in the system and is now being redistributed through space.

Sharp-edged vacuum transitions are ruled out. When a mass consumes vacuum locally, the surrounding configuration cannot have a discontinuous edge separating the consumed region from the undisturbed vacuum. A sharp edge would require infinite configuration energy, which is not available. Instead, the vacuum must smoothly transition from reduced to full over some region, and this transition region is what we observe as a gravity well. The shape of the well is determined by Postulate 5 (minimum-energy configuration given constraints), but the smoothness of the well is already forced by Postulate 4's commitment that infinities cost infinite energy.

The no-infinities commitment also has specific consequences for gravitational collapse and black hole structure. These consequences are developed in the separate document on the framework's exclusion of singularities.

## Relationship to General Relativity

General relativity recognizes that gravitational fields carry energy, though the accounting is technically difficult. The ADM energy, the Bondi energy, and various other definitions exist for specific spacetime configurations, each capturing some aspect of the energy content of a gravitational field. The positive energy theorem [Schoen & Yau, 1979; Witten, 1981] establishes that under reasonable physical conditions, the total energy of an isolated system in general relativity is non-negative, with zero achieved only by flat Minkowski space.

Our postulate adopts a simpler version of this result as a commitment appropriate to the framework's current state. Flat vacuum has zero configuration energy. All other configurations have positive configuration energy. We are not deriving the positive energy theorem from deeper principles — we are taking something like its content as an axiom for our framework.

This is analogous to how we state mass-energy equivalence explicitly (see `sr_mass_energy_equivalence.md`) even though it follows from special relativity. Having the claim explicit in our own terms lets derivations reference it directly without needing to prove results from GR along the way.

Where our framework and GR differ in philosophy: GR treats the metric as the primary geometric object and gravitational energy as a property of the metric (or of metric differences from a background). Our framework treats vacuum as the primary substance and configuration as a geometric property of the vacuum that happens to carry energy. These are compatible framings at the level of observable predictions but locate the energy differently.

## What the Postulate Does Not Do

**It does not specify how to calculate configuration energy quantitatively.** The postulate says configurations have energies, with flat at zero and curved configurations at positive values, but it does not give a formula for the energy of a specific configuration. Quantitative calculations would require resolving the open question of the vacuum's mathematical structure.

**It does not specify how configuration energy relates to mass-energy.** A mass has rest energy $mc^2$. A mass in space has a gravitational well around it, which has configuration energy. Are these two contributions separate, overlapping, or related by some equation? The postulate does not say.

**It does not commit to a positivity theorem for all physical situations.** We assert that flat has zero configuration energy and departures from flat have positive configuration energy. Whether this holds for every conceivable configuration, including exotic ones involving negative energy conditions or pathological spacetimes, is beyond the postulate's scope. We are making the claim for the configurations the framework will be working with.

**It does not say what configurations exist or are realizable.** The postulate describes what configurations would have if they existed. Which configurations are physically realized depends on the minimum-energy dynamics (addressed by the next postulate).

---

## What It Prepares For

Postulate 4 sets up the claim that configurations have energies. On its own, this is just an accounting principle. It becomes dynamically significant when combined with the next postulate, which states that the vacuum arranges itself into the lowest-energy configuration consistent with given constraints.

Together, Postulates 4 and 5 give the framework a variational principle for vacuum dynamics. Configurations have energies; systems settle into minimum-energy configurations given their constraints; departures from minimum relax toward minimum through propagating waves. Each of these is a separate commitment, but together they form the framework's account of how vacuum behaves dynamically.

Postulate 4 is the first piece of that account.
