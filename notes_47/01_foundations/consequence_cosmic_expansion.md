# Consequence: Cosmic Expansion

## Space Creation, Vacuum Creation, Energy Creation

---

## What This Document Is

This is a consequence document — it traces out what the framework says about a specific observed phenomenon, rather than introducing a new postulate or deriving a new theorem. The phenomenon is the observed expansion of the universe. The consequence is what that expansion means under Postulate 1.

The observation itself is well-established and not in dispute. What this document adds is the reinterpretation the framework forces us into: if space is vacuum is energy, then the expansion of space is the creation of energy.

We do not claim this is a new prediction. It is a reinterpretation of a known phenomenon under a different ontology. Whether the reinterpretation eventually leads to quantitative divergence from standard cosmology is an open question we do not answer here.

---

## The Observation

The universe is expanding. This has been known since Hubble's observations in 1929 and has been confirmed by every subsequent cosmological measurement [Hubble, 1929; Planck Collaboration, 2020]. Space itself grows over time. Distant galaxies recede from us, not primarily because they are moving through space, but because the space between us and them is getting larger.

The rate of expansion is also well-measured, and in 1998 it was discovered that the expansion is not merely continuing but accelerating [Riess et al., 1998; Perlmutter et al., 1999]. The standard account attributes this acceleration to dark energy, typically modeled as a cosmological constant $\Lambda$ with a specific (and surprisingly small) nonzero value.

None of this is controversial. Our framework does not dispute the observation or the basic description of it. What our framework changes is the interpretation of what expansion *is*.

---

## The Chain of Implications

Under Postulate 1, space is identical to the vacuum. Under the core postulates, the vacuum is an energetic substance — not merely a thing with energy density, but a form of energy in the same literal sense that mass is a form of energy.

These commitments, taken together, force a specific reading of cosmic expansion.

If space is expanding, more space exists now than existed before. Under Postulate 1, more space means more vacuum. Under the core postulates, more vacuum means more energy. Therefore, the expansion of the universe is the creation of energy.

Each step in this chain follows from a commitment we have already made. The chain does not require any new postulate. It is what happens when the framework we have built is applied to an observed phenomenon.

---

## Why This Is Not a Violation of Physics

The claim that energy is being created sounds, at first, like a violation of one of physics' most well-established laws. It is not. The reason it is not is worth understanding clearly.

Conservation of energy is not a fundamental law of nature in the way it is often presented in introductory physics. It is a consequence of a symmetry — specifically, time-translation symmetry, the claim that physics works the same way at different times. This connection was established by Noether's theorem [Noether, 1918]: every continuous symmetry of a physical system corresponds to a conserved quantity. Time-translation symmetry corresponds to energy conservation.

In a static universe, time-translation symmetry holds globally, and energy is globally conserved. In an expanding universe, time-translation symmetry is globally broken: the universe at one time is objectively different from the universe at a later time, because it is larger. Correspondingly, global energy conservation fails in an expanding universe. This is standard cosmology and not a consequence of our framework.

What our framework adds is a physical interpretation of where the non-conservation lives. Under standard cosmology, the question "where does the new energy come from?" is dismissed as malformed — global energy conservation simply does not apply. Under our framework, the question has an answer: the new energy comes from the new space, because space is energy.

Local energy conservation, meanwhile, continues to hold in our framework. The core postulates describe local exchanges between vacuum and kinetic energy, and those exchanges conserve energy locally. What fails globally is the summation of local energies over cosmologically large regions, and that failure corresponds to the global breakdown of time-translation symmetry in an expanding universe.

## Connection to the Cosmological Constant

The cosmological constant $\Lambda$ is observed to behave as a constant energy density of the vacuum — a fixed amount of energy per unit volume, which does not dilute as the universe expands [Planck Collaboration, 2020]. This is already strange under standard cosmology: for matter and radiation, energy density decreases as volume grows, because the same amount of matter or radiation is spread across more space. For the cosmological constant, energy density stays fixed. More volume, same density, more total energy.

Under our framework, this is not strange. If space is being created during expansion, and each newly created region of space carries its own energy (because space is energy), then the total dark energy increases in proportion to the increase in volume. The constancy of $\rho_\Lambda$ is what we would expect if expansion is the physical creation of vacuum at some characteristic density.

We are not claiming to have solved the cosmological constant problem — the question of why $\Lambda$ has the specific small value it does remains open. What we are noting is that the *qualitative* behavior of dark energy (constant density during expansion) aligns naturally with our framework in a way it does not align with a picture where the cosmological constant is the zero-point energy of fields living on a separate manifold.

This is a suggestive connection, not a derivation. We flag it because it is the most obvious place the framework might eventually make contact with quantitative cosmology, and because the alignment is too tidy to leave unnoted.

## What Remains Open

The reinterpretation offered here raises more questions than it answers.

**What causes expansion?** The framework says what expansion *is* under our ontology — the creation of vacuum and therefore of energy. It does not say what drives expansion, why the universe began expanding, or why the expansion is accelerating. A complete account would need a dynamical story for these, which we do not have.

**At what rate is vacuum created?** If expansion is vacuum creation, there should be a rate of vacuum creation that relates to observed cosmological parameters like the Hubble constant. We have not worked out this relationship.

**Does local physics feel the expansion?** Standard cosmology says that atoms, solar systems, and galaxies do not expand with the universe because they are bound by forces much stronger than the cosmological expansion rate. Our framework's answer should be the same at leading order, but whether there are subtle corrections — ways that local physics feels the ongoing creation of vacuum — is a question the framework makes natural to ask. We do not answer it here.

**Is the $\rho_v V$ accounting correct?** Throughout this document we have assumed, at least implicitly, that the total energy in a region of vacuum is proportional to its volume. This assumption has not been formally justified within the framework, and earlier documents have flagged that the quantitative relationship between vacuum density and total vacuum energy is not yet pinned down. The qualitative arguments in this document do not depend strongly on this assumption, but any quantitative development would need to address it.

**What about the early universe?** If expansion is vacuum creation, the early universe — smaller, denser, hotter — was a state with less vacuum. What this means for our framework's treatment of cosmological history, including inflation and the Big Bang itself, is entirely open.

These are directions for further work. This document's job is only to notice that the framework, applied to cosmic expansion, forces a specific reinterpretation of what expansion is, and to trace the implications of that reinterpretation far enough to see what questions it raises.
