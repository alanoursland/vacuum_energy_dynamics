# Overview

## The Supposition

---

## A Thought Experiment

In 1905, Albert Einstein asked what the world would look like if the speed of light were observed to be the same in every reference frame [Einstein, 1905a]. This was not a claim that such a thing was true. It was a supposition — a "what if" — posed precisely so that its consequences could be worked out rigorously and examined. The consequences turned out to be strange and far-reaching.

This document poses a supposition in the same spirit. It is a question of the form "what if?" — adopted provisionally so that we can reason from it and see where the reasoning leads.

The supposition is this:

> **What if the vacuum is energy, and vacuum and energy can be converted into each other in the same way mass and energy can?**

Everything in this project is an exploration of the consequences of this supposition.

---

## Why This Question

Special relativity, through the relation $E = mc^2$ [Einstein, 1905b], tells us that mass and energy are two aspects of a single underlying quantity. What looks like mass can be converted to energy, and what looks like energy can be converted to mass. This identification is one of the deepest unifications in physics.

The vacuum is known to have energy. Quantum field theory predicts an energy density from the zero-point fluctuations of fields [Weinberg, 1989]. General relativity accommodates a vacuum energy in the form of the cosmological constant, which observation now strongly suggests is nonzero [Planck Collaboration, 2020]. By various accounts, the vacuum contains energy.

The word "vacuum" has several technical meanings in contemporary physics. The *classical vacuum* is simply empty space, the absence of matter. The *quantum vacuum* is the ground state of quantum fields — not truly empty, but alive with zero-point fluctuations and virtual particle pairs, carrying a nonzero energy density. The *cosmological vacuum* is the energy density associated with the cosmological constant, observationally responsible for the accelerating expansion of the universe. These meanings are related but not interchangeable.

When we say "vacuum" in this project, we mean the quantum vacuum — the ground state of quantum fields, including the zero-point fluctuations and the energy density they carry. We take this to be physically real, not merely a calculational artifact. The classical notion of vacuum as "low air pressure extended to its limit" is not what we mean. The cosmological vacuum enters the discussion only where its relationship to the quantum vacuum is directly relevant.

The framework will later propose that this quantum vacuum is not a state of fields living on a separate spacetime manifold, but is identical to spacetime itself. That identification is the subject of its own document and is not assumed here.

But the vacuum is not usually treated as a *participant* in energy conversion. It has energy, but that energy does not flow into or out of anything. Mass and energy trade with each other; vacuum sits on the sidelines.

The supposition asks what would happen if it didn't — if the vacuum is a third convertible form, and energy can be drawn out of the vacuum by consuming it, and vacuum can be restored by pouring energy back in.

## Relationship to Special Relativity

We adopt special relativity in full [Einstein, 1905a; Einstein, 1905b]. Our supposition builds on SR rather than replacing it. In particular:

We use the constancy of the speed of light in all inertial frames. We use the relativity of simultaneity, time dilation, and length contraction. We use the Lorentz transformations as the correct relationship between inertial frames.

We make essential use of mass-energy equivalence, $E = mc^2$. Our supposition is, in a sense, an extension of this equivalence — we are proposing that the vacuum be admitted into the same family of convertible quantities as mass and energy. Without SR's identification of mass with energy, our proposal would have nothing to extend.

Any theory built on this supposition must be consistent with the experimental successes of SR. This is a constraint we accept from the outset.

---

## Relationship to General Relativity

We do not adopt general relativity. This is not a rejection — it is a methodological decision about what we are allowed to assume.

GR is the standard and extraordinarily successful theory of gravity. It has passed every experimental test to date, often to remarkable precision [Will, 2014]. Any serious framework in this area must eventually explain why GR works so well, and must be consistent with the observations that GR accounts for.

Our approach is to develop the consequences of the supposition from SR alone, without importing GR's machinery. We bracket GR not because we doubt its success, but because we want to know how much of gravitational phenomenology can be recovered from a different starting point. The goal is to see what gravitational phenomena fall out of the supposition on their own terms. Where we recover results that match GR's predictions in regimes that have been experimentally tested, we will have shown that our framework is empirically compatible with gravitational observations to date. Where we recover results that differ from GR — or from GR's untested extrapolations — we will have candidates for falsifying tests.

We expect one of three outcomes, and we do not know in advance which will occur:

The framework may turn out to be a reinterpretation of GR — mathematically equivalent, but built on a different ontological foundation. This would be a philosophical contribution rather than a physical one, and it would be valuable for giving a different intuitive account of what gravity is.

The framework may turn out to be equivalent to a known alternative gravity theory, such as a scalar-tensor theory in the Brans-Dicke family [Brans & Dicke, 1961]. This would connect our supposition to existing literature and available tools, and would locate our work within the known landscape of alternative gravity theories.

The framework may turn out to make predictions genuinely distinct from GR, in regimes where GR has been less stringently tested — strong fields, cosmological scales, or the quantum regime. This would make our framework a falsifiable rival to GR. The outcome of such tests would settle whether the supposition corresponds to anything real.

We do not expect to overturn GR in its regime of established validity. We do hope to rederive the phenomena GR describes from a different starting point, and we are open to finding places where our framework and GR diverge.

## How We Proceed

From the supposition, we develop a small number of postulates that make the "what if" precise enough to reason from. From the postulates, we derive what we can. When reasoning exposes the need for additional commitments, we add them explicitly and examine them on their own terms, rather than letting them enter quietly.

Each postulate and each derivation is captured in its own document. The README maintains the overall structure and shows how the pieces relate. This document is the entry point: everything else assumes the spirit of provisional supposition established here.
