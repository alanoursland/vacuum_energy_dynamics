# Postulate 1: Vacuum-Spacetime Identity

---

## The Question

The core postulates of this framework treat the vacuum as something with energy density that can be exchanged with kinetic energy. Energy descends through a vacuum gradient and gains kinetic energy, consuming vacuum. Energy ascends against a vacuum gradient and loses kinetic energy, regenerating vacuum.

Those postulates describe what the vacuum *does*. They do not yet say what the vacuum *is*, or how it relates to the spacetime in which physics happens. This document addresses that question.

The answer we adopt is that the vacuum and spacetime are not two things but one. This document lays out the options we considered, explains why we chose identity over the alternatives, and spells out what the commitment buys the framework and what it still leaves open.

---

## Defining Spacetime Functionally

Before we can ask how vacuum relates to spacetime, we need a working definition of spacetime. We adopt a functional one.

**Spacetime is the structure that constrains how information can propagate.**

By "information" we do not mean the formalism of information theory in any narrow sense. We mean the physical notion of one event influencing another — a signal that can be sent, a force that can be felt, a correlation that can be detected. Spacetime is what determines, for any two events, whether one can affect the other, and if so, how quickly and by what paths.

In practice, information of this kind is realized through energy transfer. Detecting anything requires energy to reach the detector. Influencing anything requires energy to be delivered to it. The speed limit $c$ applies fundamentally to information — entangled correlations, phase velocities, and cosmological expansion can all move faster than $c$ in various senses, but none of them transmit information — and $c$ applies to energy transfer because energy transfer is how information normally propagates.

This functional definition is not controversial. It is what spacetime does in every physical theory we have, regardless of what that theory says spacetime *is*. We take it as our starting point.

---

## The Options

Given the functional definition of spacetime, and given that our core postulates treat the vacuum as a participant in energy exchanges, there are four ways the vacuum and spacetime could be related.

**Option 1: The vacuum is a field on a separate spacetime.** This is the standard picture in quantum field theory on curved spacetime. The spacetime manifold is one thing, with its own geometric structure. The vacuum is the ground state of quantum fields defined on that manifold. They are ontologically distinct. The vacuum has energy, and that energy sources spacetime curvature through something like the Einstein equations, but the vacuum and the manifold remain two different things.

**Option 2: The vacuum is coupled to spacetime.** Vacuum and spacetime are distinct things that influence each other through a coupling. When vacuum changes at a location, the spacetime geometry responds according to the coupling's strength and dynamics. Scalar-tensor gravity theories like Brans-Dicke [Brans & Dicke, 1961] operate in this territory: a scalar field is coupled to the spacetime metric, and the two evolve together but remain distinguishable.

**Option 3: The vacuum is identical to spacetime.** Vacuum and spacetime are not two things. They are one substance described from two angles — the energy-and-exchange angle (what quantum field theory calls the vacuum) and the geometric-constraint-on-propagation angle (what general relativity calls spacetime). A change in one is a change in the other, not because of a coupling but because they are the same change.

**Option 4: Spacetime is emergent from vacuum.** The vacuum is more fundamental, and spacetime emerges from it as a derived phenomenon with its own degrees of freedom at a more basic level. Entropic gravity [Verlinde, 2011], thermodynamic derivations of the Einstein equations [Jacobson, 1995], and causal set approaches [Bombelli et al., 1987] live in this territory.

---

## Why Option 3

We adopt Option 3. Several reasons push us in that direction.

**Options 1 and 2 require machinery we have not committed to.** If the vacuum and spacetime are distinct, we need an account of how vacuum changes produce spacetime changes. This means either importing Einstein's field equations (which is adopting general relativity, something we have explicitly bracketed) or specifying a coupling, which is one or more additional postulates with free parameters. Either move increases the framework's complexity without gain.

**Option 4 requires a substructure we have not developed.** An emergent-spacetime picture is coherent, but it demands that we posit what the vacuum is made of at a deeper level and show how spacetime arises from that substructure. We have no such theory. Option 4 is a plausible direction for future work, but it is not where we are.

**Option 3 gives us simplifying assumptions.** This is the most important reason, and it deserves emphasis.

Under Option 2, vacuum-energy exchanges and spacetime-geometry changes would be two events connected by a coupling. We would need a coupling constant, a relaxation timescale, a separate equation of motion for the spacetime response, and a story about how energy exchanges get mediated into geometric changes. Each of these would be a free parameter or an additional postulate that we would have to pin down — and that later data would have to constrain.

Under Option 3, all of these collapse. Vacuum-energy exchange and the corresponding spacetime change are one event, described in two ways. There is no coupling to be strong or weak. There is no mediation delay. There is no separate dynamics for the geometric response. What our core postulates describe as energy exchanges with the vacuum are, under the identity, also the changes in spacetime structure that produce observable gravitational effects.

This is the postulate doing real work. It does not just rename the vacuum as spacetime. It removes degrees of freedom that would otherwise be free parameters, making the framework more predictive rather than less.

---

## What the Identity Forbids, and What It Permits

The identity is a specific commitment with specific consequences. Naming what it rules out is as important as naming what it allows.

**The identity forbids** any account in which vacuum changes and spacetime changes are separable dynamical events. They cannot lag, cannot decouple, cannot have independent dynamics. An ascending photon that converts kinetic energy to vacuum is not first changing the vacuum and then, through some mediation, changing the spacetime. It is doing one thing, which can be described either as vacuum regeneration or as a change in the information-propagation structure of the region — but the two descriptions name one event.

**The identity permits** descriptive frameworks that decompose the identified substance into components for particular purposes. A scalar-tensor formalism might turn out to be a useful way of describing the dynamics computationally, provided that in the limit where the theory is applied it collapses to a single substance. An emergent-spacetime approach might turn out to derive the identity from deeper structure, without contradicting it. What the identity demands is that any such decomposition ultimately points at one substance, not two. Descriptions are free; ontology is one.

This generosity is real but limited. It is not an invitation to reintroduce Option 1 or Option 2 through the back door. It is an acknowledgment that a framework stated at one level may be reformulated at another, and that a good reformulation preserves the identity even while analyzing its structure.

---

## The Postulate

On the basis of the above, we adopt the following.

**Postulate 1 (Vacuum-Spacetime Identity).** The vacuum, as characterized by its energy density and its capacity for energy exchange, is identical to spacetime, which is the structure that constrains how information can propagate. A change in vacuum energy at a location is a change in the information-propagation structure at that location, described from a different angle. The two are not two events in a dynamical relationship. They are one event.

A corollary worth stating directly and without softening: since the core postulates commit to the vacuum being energy, and the identity makes the vacuum equal to spacetime, the postulate commits us to the view that **spacetime is energy**.

This claim is meant literally, in the same sense that mass is energy. $E = mc^2$ is not a metaphor, not an accounting convention, and not a statement about how mass is described in some formalism. It is a claim that mass and energy are the same thing in different forms, with specific conversion processes between them. Our postulate makes the same kind of claim about spacetime. Spacetime and energy are the same thing in different forms.

A reader who translates "spacetime is energy" into "spacetime has an energy density," "spacetime couples to energy through a stress-energy tensor," or "spacetime is described by an energy term in some Lagrangian" has not read the postulate correctly. Those are all claims modern physics already makes, none of which require our framework. The postulate is stronger: there is no spacetime in addition to energy. The substance that constrains information propagation and the substance that participates in energy exchanges are the same substance, not two things that happen to be related.

---

## What the Postulate Buys

Adopting the identity gives the framework several concrete consequences.

**Energy conservation becomes complete when the vacuum is included.** The core postulates already commit to the vacuum being a convertible form of energy. Under the identity, the vacuum is also the spacetime substrate. Taken together, these commitments imply that any complete accounting of energy in a physical process must include the energy stored in and exchanged with the spacetime itself. Processes that appear to violate energy conservation under a ledger that excludes the vacuum may be perfectly conservative once the vacuum is counted. Conversely, processes that do not involve vacuum exchange work the same way they always have. The framework does not relax energy conservation; it widens the ledger.

This principle is general but not automatic. Including the vacuum in the ledger is a candidate mechanism for any apparent energy-conservation puzzle, not an instant resolution. To apply the principle, one must specify when vacuum exchange occurs in a given process and how much vacuum is exchanged. Without those specifics, invoking the vacuum explains nothing — it only names where an explanation might live. The framework commits us to counting the vacuum; it does not exempt us from showing our work.

**Removed free parameters.** Under any coupling-based alternative, the framework would carry additional free parameters controlling the coupling's strength and dynamics. The identity removes them. The dynamics of spacetime are the dynamics of the vacuum. There is nothing else to pin down.

**An ontological basis for later derivations.** Several questions the framework will eventually face — the spatial structure of vacuum density around sources, the equations of motion for test bodies, the behavior of gravitational radiation — become easier to pose under the identity. They become questions about a single substance rather than about two coupled things. Whether they become easier to *answer* depends on further work, but they become easier to *formulate*.

---

## What the Postulate Does Not Do

The identity does not give us everything. It is important to be clear about what remains open.

**The mathematical structure of the vacuum is not specified.** The postulate says the vacuum is the spacetime substrate, but it does not say whether the vacuum is a scalar field, a tensor field, or something richer. The functional argument suggests the vacuum must have enough structure to implement all of spacetime's roles — metric, causal order, dimension — which likely exceeds what a simple scalar density can provide. This is open work.

**The spatial profile of vacuum density around sources is not derived.** The postulate tells us the local geometry tracks the local vacuum, but not what the vacuum density looks like around a given configuration. Deriving that is a separate project.

**The connection to Einstein's equations is not established.** The identity is compatible with recovering Einstein's equations as a consequence, and it is compatible with producing something different. Which occurs depends on further work.

**A quantum formulation is not provided.** The postulate is classical in character. How the identity looks in a quantum regime — where spacetime itself may need to be quantized along with the vacuum — is beyond the current scope.

## Adjacent Programs

Several existing research programs share some of the spirit of our postulate while differing in commitments and approach.

Entropic gravity [Verlinde, 2011] derives gravitational phenomena from information-theoretic considerations applied to holographic screens. It shares our intuition that gravity connects to information propagation, but operates by deriving gravitational dynamics from entropy gradients rather than by identifying a substance with the information-propagation substrate.

Thermodynamic derivations of the Einstein equations [Jacobson, 1995] treat the field equations as emerging from a Clausius relation applied to causal horizons. This is in the spirit of Option 4 in our list — spacetime dynamics as emergent from deeper thermodynamic principles — and is an interesting candidate for how our Option 3 identity might eventually be derived from a more fundamental theory.

Causal set theory [Bombelli et al., 1987] takes causal structure as primary and treats spacetime as emergent from a discrete set of causally related events. This shares our view that causal structure is fundamental, but commits to a discrete substructure we do not adopt.

We note these adjacencies because our framework is not operating in isolation. Whether it converges with any of these programs, diverges from them, or turns out to be a reformulation of one of them is a question that requires further development of both our framework and a closer reading of theirs than we have undertaken here.
