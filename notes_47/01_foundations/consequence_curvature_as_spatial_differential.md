# Consequence: Curvature as Spatial Volume Differential

---

## What This Document Is

This is a consequence document. It traces out what the framework's existing postulates commit us to say about the geometric nature of curvature, without introducing new postulates. The postulates involved are Postulate 2 (vacuum has locally constant finite energy density) and Postulate 1 (vacuum is the substrate that constrains information propagation; vacuum is spacetime).

The conclusion is that, under these postulates, spacetime curvature can only be a differential in the amount of vacuum contained in neighboring spans of space. It cannot be variation in density (Postulate 2 forbids this). It cannot be variation in some property separate from vacuum (Postulate 1 rules out such separation). The only remaining possibility is that curvature *is* the spatial-volume differential between neighboring spans.

This is a structural consequence, not a new commitment. It follows from what the framework has already said. We write it out explicitly because the consequence is significant enough to deserve its own treatment, and because it was previously bundled into Postulate 3 in a way that made the postulate's scope unclear.

---

## The Reasoning

Observation: spacetime is curved in the vicinity of mass. This is among the most well-verified facts in physics, observable through gravitational lensing, perihelion precession, gravitational time dilation, and the geometric structure of the solar system as mapped by precision experiments.

Our framework must account for this curvature using the substance it has already committed to — the vacuum. The question is: what kind of variation in the vacuum produces curvature?

Option A: Variation in density. In a region with higher curvature, the vacuum has higher or lower energy density than in flatter regions. This would make curvature a density phenomenon.

This option is ruled out by Postulate 2. The vacuum has locally constant energy density. Every observer at every location measures the same finite value. There is no variation in density to correspond to curvature.

Option B: Variation in a property separate from the vacuum. Some other physical quantity — a field, a tensor, a geometric structure distinct from the vacuum itself — varies, and that variation is what curvature is.

This option is ruled out by Postulate 1. Vacuum is spacetime. Under Postulate 1, spacetime does not have properties separate from the vacuum; its properties are the vacuum's properties. There is no "separate geometric structure" that could vary independently of the vacuum.

Option C: Variation in the amount of vacuum per span. Neighboring spans of space contain different amounts of vacuum, even though each span's local density is the same. The differential between spans is what we observe as curvature.

This is the only option consistent with both postulates. Curvature, in our framework, must be a spatial-volume differential.

---

## What "Amount of Vacuum" Means

The phrase is awkward in ordinary language because it invites a container-and-contents picture that the framework rejects. The vacuum is not a quantity distributed within a pre-existing container called "space"; the vacuum is the space. When we speak of "amount of vacuum in a span," we are pointing at a property of the span itself — how much space the span contains, as measured against other spans.

Analogy may help, with the usual caveat that analogies can mislead. Consider a rubber sheet that can be stretched or compressed. If the sheet has uniform density everywhere, differences between regions can only show up as differences in how much material is present — a compressed region has less material than a stretched one, even though both have the same density. The compression is an extensive property (how much of the stuff is there) rather than an intensive property (how dense it is per unit volume). Our framework's situation is analogous: the vacuum has uniform density, and spatial variation lives entirely in the extensive property of how much vacuum is present in a given span.

The disanalogy, important to name, is that a rubber sheet is stretched within a pre-existing three-dimensional space. Our spans have nothing external to them — the vacuum is the space. The stretching-and-compression language is pointing at something but the actual situation is more primitive than any stretching-within-a-container picture can fully capture. We flag this in Postulate 3's document under the language note, and the same caveat applies here.

## Consequences for Vacuum Exchange

Postulate 3 commits to vacuum being consumed during energy descent through a gradient and regenerated during energy ascent. Under Postulate 1, these exchanges are spacetime changes. Combined with the reasoning above, this produces specific consequences for curvature.

**Consumption increases local curvature.** When a mass descends through a gradient and consumes some vacuum, the consumed vacuum is removed from the region of descent. The span the mass occupied contains less vacuum than it did before the descent. Under the reasoning of this document, less vacuum per span corresponds to greater curvature. So consumption steepens the curvature locally.

**Regeneration decreases local curvature.** When a photon ascends through a gradient and regenerates vacuum, the region gains vacuum. More vacuum per span means flatter geometry. The curvature relaxes.

These are symmetric. Consumption is curvature-increasing; regeneration is curvature-decreasing. The exchange processes of Postulate 3, combined with the reasoning of this document, give us a specific geometric consequence for each direction of motion through a gradient.

## Consistency with the Stitched-Sheet Picture

Early in the framework's development we used a stitched-sheet picture to visualize curvature. Remove a disk from a flat sheet, sew the edges together, and the sheet must pucker to accommodate the missing area. The pucker is curvature produced purely by removing material, without any force applied.

This picture is consistent with the consequence stated here. Removing material is the visualization of consuming vacuum. The resulting pucker is the geometric consequence of less vacuum being present in the stitched region. Adding material back (regenerating vacuum) would flatten the sheet.

The stitched-sheet picture captures the angular compression aspect of curvature cleanly — when area is removed, less circumferential distance is available at a given radius, and this is what produces the pucker. The picture is less intuitive about radial stretching, which near an actual mass shows up as proper radial distances being longer than the external geometry suggests. A mass falling into a well traverses more proper distance than the coordinate description indicates.

Both aspects — angular compression and radial stretching — are consequences of the same underlying fact: less vacuum per span in the curved region. How they combine geometrically is a question the framework would need to work out in more detail, but the qualitative picture is consistent.

## What This Consequence Does Not Do

It does not derive quantitative curvature from quantitative vacuum consumption. The relationship between "how much vacuum is consumed" and "how curved the resulting region is" depends on the mathematical structure of the vacuum, which remains an open question of the framework. Whether the relationship is linear, nonlinear, or involves additional structure we have not committed to, cannot be determined here.

It does not reproduce the Einstein field equations. GR relates curvature to stress-energy through a specific tensorial relationship. Our framework commits to curvature being a spatial-volume differential, but does not yet specify how it connects to energy content or to the exchange processes of Postulate 3 in enough detail to reproduce GR's specific field equations. Whether our framework ultimately recovers GR's equations, produces corrections to them, or diverges from them in some regime is unresolved.

It does not specify the geometric structure of spans. We have not said whether spans are discrete or continuous, whether they have a particular shape, or how they relate to coordinates used by observers in different frames. The graph-like picture mentioned in Postulate 3's language note is one possibility; a continuous substructure is another; something we have not yet imagined is a third. The consequence stated here is compatible with several possibilities and commits to none of them.

It does not address what gravity is at a mechanistic level. Postulate 3 explicitly declines to propose a mechanism for the gravitational force, and nothing in this consequence document changes that. We have said what curvature *is*, in terms of the framework's substances; we have not said what produces it or how it acts on energy beyond what Postulate 3 already commits to.

---

## Where This Leaves the Framework

This consequence document makes explicit a claim that was previously bundled into Postulate 3, cleaning up the postulate's scope. The claim is structural: under the postulates we have already adopted, curvature can only be a spatial-volume differential between neighboring spans.

Several open questions remain. The mathematical structure of the vacuum is not resolved. The mechanism for gravitational force is not proposed. The connection to GR's field equations is not established. The correct picture of what spans are — discrete, continuous, or otherwise — is not chosen.

What we have gained is a cleaner understanding of what the framework is committed to about curvature. Not variation in density, not variation in some separate structure, but variation in how much vacuum exists in neighboring spans. The framework's distinctive claim about geometry lives in this extensive-rather-than-intensive variation, and this document puts that claim explicitly in its own place in the tree.
