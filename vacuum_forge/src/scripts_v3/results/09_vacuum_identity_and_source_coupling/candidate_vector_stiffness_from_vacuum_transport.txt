Case 0: Vector stiffness from vacuum transport problem
========================================================================================================================
Problem:

  W_i has a continuity-constrained source j_i = rho v_i,
  but its stiffness K_W is missing.

Question:

  Does vacuum transport suggest a vector stiffness K_W?

Allowed outcomes:

  K_W related to scalar stiffness K_A
  K_W independent but ontology-justified
  K_W missing / phenomenological

========================================================================================================================
Case 1: Scalar exchange energy reference
========================================================================================================================
Scalar reduced energy density:

  E_A ~ K_A |grad A|^2

1D form = K_A*Derivative(A(x), x)**2

K_A measures scalar exchange stiffness.

========================================================================================================================
Case 2: Candidate vector transport energy
========================================================================================================================
Candidate vector transport energy density:

  E_W ~ K_W |grad W_i|^2

1D schematic = K_W*Derivative(W(x), x)**2

If varied with source alpha_W j_i W_i, this yields:

  Delta W_i ~ - (alpha_W/K_W) j_i

But K_W is not yet derived.

========================================================================================================================
Case 3: Shared scalar/vector stiffness option
========================================================================================================================
Shared-stiffness parameterization:

Eq(K_W, K_A*lambda_K)

Special case:

  lambda_K = 1 gives K_W = K_A

Interpretation:
  scalar exchange and vector transport are two modes of the same vacuum
  stiffness only if lambda_K is derived or fixed by ontology.

========================================================================================================================
Case 4: Independent vector transport stiffness option
========================================================================================================================
Independent option:

  K_W is a distinct vector-transport stiffness.

This may be legitimate if the ontology says:

  scalar compression/exchange and vector circulation/transport cost
  different vacuum energies.

But if K_W is introduced only to fit frame dragging, that is hand matching.

========================================================================================================================
Case 5: Curl-energy option
========================================================================================================================
A more gauge-aware vector energy may use:

  E_W ~ K_c |curl W|^2

rather than:

  E_W ~ K_W |grad W|^2

Reason:
  curl W removes pure-gradient gauge-like pieces.

Open issue:
  variation of |curl W|^2 gives a transverse vector equation,
  but gauge fixing and boundary conditions are needed.

========================================================================================================================
Case 6: Source coupling energy
========================================================================================================================
Candidate source coupling:

  E_source ~ alpha_W j_i W_i

1D symbolic term = W*alpha_W*j

Interpretation:
  alpha_W controls how matter current exchanges with vector vacuum transport.

Missing:
  alpha_W is no more derived than K_W.

========================================================================================================================
Case 7: Dimensionless stiffness/coupling ratio
========================================================================================================================
Only the ratio enters the reduced source equation:

  Delta W_i = - (alpha_W/K_W) j_i

ratio = alpha_W/K_W

Therefore deriving K_W alone is not enough.
Need either:

  alpha_W and K_W separately
  or their ratio directly
