# P2: Vacuum-Spacetime Identity

## What This Postulate Says

The vacuum is identical to spacetime. They are not two things that influence each other through some coupling. They are not two descriptions of two distinct substances that happen to be related. They are one substance, described from two angles: as energy (under P1) and as the geometric structure that constrains how information can propagate.

A change in the vacuum at a location is a change in the spacetime at that location, not because of any mediation but because they are the same change.

Combined with P1's identification of vacuum with energy, this postulate commits the framework to a stronger claim: spacetime is energy. The substance that constrains information propagation and the substance that participates in energy exchanges are one substance. Spacetime can be created or destroyed through energy transfer, in the same literal sense that mass can be created or destroyed through energy transfer.

---

## The Question This Answers

P1 commits the framework to the identification of vacuum with energy. It establishes that vacuum has reality and energy content as a foundational supposition. But P1 alone does not say what the vacuum *is* in relation to the spacetime in which physics happens.

There are several ways the vacuum could relate to spacetime, and the framework's other content depends on which of these we adopt. P2 makes the choice.

---

## Defining Spacetime Functionally

Before we can ask how vacuum relates to spacetime, we need a working definition of spacetime. We adopt a functional one.

**Spacetime is the structure that constrains how information can propagate.**

By "information" we do not mean the formalism of information theory in any narrow sense. We mean the physical notion of one event influencing another — a signal that can be sent, a force that can be felt, a correlation that can be detected. Spacetime is what determines, for any two events, whether one can affect the other, and if so, how quickly and by what paths.

In practice, information of this kind is realized through energy transfer. Detecting anything requires energy to reach the detector. Influencing anything requires energy to be delivered to it. The speed limit $c$ (SR2) applies fundamentally to information — entangled correlations, phase velocities, and cosmological expansion can all move faster than $c$ in various senses, but none of them transmit information — and $c$ applies to energy transfer because energy transfer is how information normally propagates.

This functional definition is not controversial. It is what spacetime does in every physical theory we have, regardless of what that theory says spacetime *is*. We take it as our starting point.

---

## The Options

Given the functional definition of spacetime, and given that P1 commits the framework to vacuum being energy, there are four ways the vacuum and spacetime could be related.

**Option 1: The vacuum is a field on a separate spacetime.** This is the standard picture in quantum field theory on curved spacetime. The spacetime manifold is one thing, with its own geometric structure. The vacuum is the ground state of quantum fields defined on that manifold. They are ontologically distinct. The vacuum has energy, and that energy sources spacetime curvature through something like the Einstein equations, but the vacuum and the manifold remain two different things.

**Option 2: The vacuum is coupled to spacetime.** Vacuum and spacetime are distinct things that influence each other through a coupling. When vacuum changes at a location, the spacetime geometry responds according to the coupling's strength and dynamics. Scalar-tensor gravity theories like Brans-Dicke [Brans & Dicke, 1961] operate in this territory: a scalar field is coupled to the spacetime metric, and the two evolve together but remain distinguishable.

**Option 3: The vacuum is identical to spacetime.** Vacuum and spacetime are not two things. They are one substance described from two angles — the energy-and-exchange angle (what P1 commits to) and the geometric-constraint-on-propagation angle (the functional definition of spacetime). A change in one is a change in the other, not because of a coupling but because they are the same change.

**Option 4: Spacetime is emergent from vacuum.** The vacuum is more fundamental, and spacetime emerges from it as a derived phenomenon with its own degrees of freedom at a more basic level. Entropic gravity [Verlinde, 2011], thermodynamic derivations of the Einstein equations [Jacobson, 1995], and causal set approaches [Bombelli et al., 1987] live in this territory.

---

## Why Option 3

We adopt Option 3. Several reasons push us in that direction.

**Options 1 and 2 require machinery we have not committed to.** If the vacuum and spacetime are distinct, we need an account of how vacuum changes produce spacetime changes. This means either importing Einstein's field equations (which would be adopting general relativity wholesale, something the framework has explicitly bracketed) or specifying a coupling, which is one or more additional postulates with free parameters. Either move increases the framework's complexity without compensating gain.

**Option 4 requires a substructure we have not developed.** An emergent-spacetime picture is coherent, but it demands that we posit what the vacuum is made of at a deeper level and show how spacetime arises from that substructure. We have no such theory. Option 4 is a plausible direction for future work, but it is not where we are.

**Option 3 gives us simplifying assumptions.** This is the most important reason, and it deserves emphasis.

Under Option 2, vacuum-energy exchanges and spacetime-geometry changes would be two events connected by a coupling. We would need a coupling constant, a relaxation timescale, a separate equation of motion for the spacetime response, and a story about how energy exchanges get mediated into geometric changes. Each of these would be a free parameter or an additional postulate that we would have to pin down — and that later data would have to constrain.

Under Option 3, all of these collapse. Vacuum-energy exchange and the corresponding spacetime change are one event, described in two ways. There is no coupling to be strong or weak. There is no mediation delay. There is no separate dynamics for the geometric response. Energy exchanges with the vacuum are, under the identity, also changes in spacetime structure.

This is the postulate doing real work. It does not just rename the vacuum as spacetime. It removes degrees of freedom that would otherwise be free parameters, making the framework more predictive rather than less.

---

## What the Identity Forbids, and What It Permits

The identity is a specific commitment with specific consequences. Naming what it rules out is as important as naming what it allows.

**The identity forbids** any account in which vacuum changes and spacetime changes are separable dynamical events. They cannot lag, cannot decouple, cannot have independent dynamics. An event described in framework-native vocabulary as "vacuum is destroyed" is also described as "spacetime is destroyed" — one event, two descriptions, not two events with a relationship between them.

**The identity permits** descriptive frameworks that decompose the identified substance into components for particular purposes. A scalar-tensor formalism might turn out to be a useful way of describing the dynamics computationally, provided that in the limit where the theory is applied it collapses to a single substance. An emergent-spacetime approach might turn out to derive the identity from deeper structure, without contradicting it. What the identity demands is that any such decomposition ultimately points at one substance, not two. Descriptions are free; ontology is one.

This generosity is real but limited. It is not an invitation to reintroduce Option 1 or Option 2 through the back door. It is an acknowledgment that a framework stated at one level may be reformulated at another, and that a good reformulation preserves the identity even while analyzing its structure.

---

## The Postulate

On the basis of the above, we adopt the following.

**P2 (Vacuum-Spacetime Identity).** The vacuum, as committed to by P1, is identical to spacetime, the structure that constrains how information can propagate. A change in vacuum at a location is a change in the information-propagation structure at that location, described from a different angle. The two are not two events in a dynamical relationship. They are one event.

A corollary worth stating directly: combined with P1, the postulate commits the framework to the view that **spacetime is energy**.

This claim is meant literally, in the same sense (SR4) that mass is energy. $E = mc^2$ is not a metaphor, not an accounting convention, and not a statement about how mass is described in some formalism. It is a claim that mass and energy are the same thing in different forms, with specific conversion processes between them. Our postulate makes the same kind of claim about spacetime. Spacetime and energy are the same thing in different forms.

A reader who translates "spacetime is energy" into "spacetime has an energy density," "spacetime couples to energy through a stress-energy tensor," or "spacetime is described by an energy term in some Lagrangian" has not read the postulate correctly. Those are all claims modern physics already makes, none of which require our framework. The postulate is stronger: there is no spacetime in addition to energy. The substance that constrains information propagation and the substance that participates in energy exchanges are the same substance, not two things that happen to be related.

---

## Spacetime Can Be Created and Destroyed

A direct consequence of P1 and P2 together is that spacetime is not conserved.

Under P1, vacuum is energy. Under P2, vacuum is spacetime. Therefore spacetime is energy, and energy transfer involving vacuum is also spacetime transfer.

Specifically: when a process destroys vacuum (consumes vacuum energy and converts it into another form, such as kinetic energy of a falling body), that process also destroys spacetime. The location where vacuum was consumed is no longer part of the spacetime in the way it was before — there is less spacetime there. Conversely, when a process creates vacuum, that process also creates spacetime — there is more spacetime than there was before.

This is a strong claim and it deserves to be stated explicitly. The framework commits to spacetime being a substance that can come into and go out of existence through energy-exchange processes. The total amount of spacetime in a region is not a conserved quantity; it changes through specific physical processes.

---

## What the Postulate Buys

Adopting the identity gives the framework several concrete consequences.

**Removed free parameters.** Under any coupling-based alternative, the framework would carry additional free parameters controlling the coupling's strength and dynamics. The identity removes them. The dynamics of spacetime are the dynamics of the vacuum. There is nothing else to pin down.

**Energy conservation becomes complete when the vacuum is included.** P1 already commits to the vacuum being a form of energy. Under P2, the vacuum is also the spacetime substrate. Taken together, these commitments imply that any complete accounting of energy in a physical process must include the energy stored in and exchanged with the spacetime itself. Processes that appear to violate energy conservation under a ledger that excludes the vacuum may be perfectly conservative once the vacuum is counted. Conversely, processes that do not involve vacuum exchange work the same way they always have. The framework does not relax energy conservation; it widens the ledger.

This principle is general but not automatic. Including the vacuum in the ledger is a candidate mechanism for any apparent energy-conservation puzzle, not an instant resolution. To apply the principle, one must specify when vacuum exchange occurs in a given process and how much vacuum is exchanged. Without those specifics, invoking the vacuum explains nothing — it only names where an explanation might live. The framework commits us to counting the vacuum; it does not exempt us from showing our work.

**An ontological basis for later derivations.** Several questions the framework will eventually face — the spatial structure of vacuum around sources, the equations of motion for test bodies, the behavior of gravitational radiation — become easier to pose under the identity. They become questions about a single substance rather than about two coupled things. Whether they become easier to *answer* depends on further work, but they become easier to *formulate*.

---

## What the Postulate Does Not Do

The identity does not give us everything. It is important to be clear about what remains open.

**The mathematical structure of the vacuum-spacetime is not specified.** The postulate says the vacuum is the spacetime substrate, but it does not say whether the vacuum is a scalar field, a tensor field, or something richer. The functional argument suggests the vacuum must have enough structure to implement all of spacetime's roles — metric, causal order, dimension — which likely exceeds what a simple scalar density can provide. This is open work, addressed in part by later postulates and in part by future development.

**The spatial profile of vacuum around sources is not derived.** P2 tells us the local geometry tracks the local vacuum, but not what the vacuum looks like around a given mass configuration. Deriving that is a separate project.

**A quantum formulation is not provided.** The postulate is classical in character. How the identity looks in a quantum regime — where spacetime itself may need to be quantized along with the vacuum — is beyond the current scope.

**The conversion mechanism between vacuum and other forms of energy is not specified.** P2 commits to spacetime being convertible with energy through the same kinds of processes that govern any energy conversion. But the specific physical mechanisms — what causes vacuum to be destroyed in a given interaction, how much is destroyed, what triggers creation versus destruction — are addressed by later postulates and remain partly open work.

## Adjacent Programs

Several existing research programs share some of the spirit of P2 while differing in commitments and approach.

Entropic gravity [Verlinde, 2011] derives gravitational phenomena from information-theoretic considerations applied to holographic screens. It shares the framework's intuition that gravity connects to information propagation, but operates by deriving gravitational dynamics from entropy gradients rather than by identifying a substance with the information-propagation substrate.

Thermodynamic derivations of the Einstein equations [Jacobson, 1995] treat the field equations as emerging from a Clausius relation applied to causal horizons. This is in the spirit of Option 4 — spacetime dynamics as emergent from deeper thermodynamic principles — and is an interesting candidate for how the framework's Option 3 identity might eventually be derived from a more fundamental theory.

Causal set theory [Bombelli et al., 1987] takes causal structure as primary and treats spacetime as emergent from a discrete set of causally related events. This shares the framework's view that causal structure is fundamental, but commits to a discrete substructure we do not adopt.

Substantivalist interpretations of general relativity treat spacetime as a substance rather than a relational structure. P2 is a substantivalist commitment in this sense, but a strengthened one: the substance is identified with energy through P1, which standard substantivalism does not require.

We note these adjacencies because the framework is not operating in isolation. Whether it converges with any of these programs, diverges from them, or turns out to be a reformulation of one of them is a question that requires further development of both the framework and a closer reading of these adjacent programs than we have undertaken here.
